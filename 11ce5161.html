Nuclear structure and organization

The origin of the nucleus is probably due to the incorporation of a second ancient procaryotic cell, with later mechanisms of membrane fusion.

The nuclear envelop is composed of two membranes. The space between is called the perinuclear space. On the surface of the external membrane there are ribosomes. The external one is continuous with the endoplasmic reticulum.

There are pores between both membranes, created by proteins forming the NPC (nuclear pore complex). There is a basket on the nuclear side, and filaments on the external one.

Transport through NPCs

Nucleoplasmin is a multimeric protein. An experiment used a radioactive nucleoplasmin. In cases where the complete nucleoplasmin was injected to the cell, the whole protein was transported to the nucleus, as well as when injecting only the nucleoplasmin tails. But when injecting nucleoplasmin bodies there was no transport. This pointed to a signaling mechanism that sitted on the tails of nucleoplasmin. Gold-binded nucleoplasmin allowed the observation of these NPCs. So, between these two compartiments the transport happens only through NPCs, and not in vesicles.

Now, there was a sequence that determined the nuclear destination of some proteins. This sequence was …-Lys-Lys-Lys-Arg-Lys-… and is called nuclear signal location (NSL).

NPC structure

There is an octamere protein ring sitting on both membranes, filaments to the outside, and a basket internally.

Nucleoporins (NPU), the structural proteins of the NPCs, is a family of proteins. There are stable and dynamic NUPs, where the stable ones form the ring.

Diffusion

Passive diffusion to the nucleus allows only small molecules. Nevertheles, the more common type of transport is the active one. The active transport uses a complex, called the importin complex with two proteins alpha and beta (check this out).

RNA is exported outside the nucleus through the same NPCs. This is made using a nuclear export receptor wich binds to NPCs and allows for the exit of the molecules.

Nuclear NPC functions

Condensed chromatin is close to the inner membrane, but there are no condensed chromatin close to NPCs.

The nuclear lamina is a protein polymer that lines the inner surface of the inner membrane. This protein allows for the association of chromatin to itslef. Nevertheless, there are some factors that modifies the gene expression at this sites.

NUPs, then can participate in the regulation of some gene expressions, splicing, etc. This means that NUPs is not only related to transport but also to gene expression and regulation.

The functions, then, are:

  • NPC-mediated gene activation (associated to the internal membrane).
  • NPC-mediated gene repression (associated to the internal membrane).
  • Intranuclear NUP-mediated gene regulation (intranuclear function).
  • NUP-mediated post-transcriptional regulation (intranuclear function).

Chromatin

There are two type of chromatin fibers: solenoidal and nucleosomic.

Nucleosoma is a complex of eight histonaes, which is a coupling of two tetrameres. It widht is 11nm, and its lenght 5nm. The DNA is curled around the complex, allowing for 146 bp of lentgh.

Histona H1 is located between two neclusoma complex. The rest od the complex is composed of H2A, H2B, H3 and H4 histonas. THey leave trenches where the DNA sits and get protected. H3 histone leaves a hook for DNA expansion.

Nuclear territory

Chromosomes are not mixed in the nucleus when being chromatin, but rather occupying a defined localization. These localizations is called the nuclear or chromosome territory. Active genes move outside the compactified chromtin form (expansion of chromatin) and moves towards the center of the nucleus for processing. Transcription association domains (TAD) allow for this process.

Hetero- and euchromatin of chromatin

Chromatin binds to the internal nuclear membrane to a protein called the nuclear lamina. The condensed chromatin binded to the lamina, also called heterochromatin (a very tight compactified form of chromatin, that is of course inactive).

Bi and trimethylation of Lysine 9 of H3 histone allows for the formation of heterochromatine. HP1 and LBR are involved proteins in this process.

Monomethylation of Lysine 4 or acetylation of Lysine 9 of H3 histone produces the transformation to euchromatin.

So, it is the DNA the one that moves towards the factory locations inside the nucleus, and not the other way around.

A pluripotent cell has very little heterochromatin, allowing for the expression of many genes. A situation where there is a lot of condensation determines a terminal cell, where there are minimal gene expressions. Progeria is due to a mutation of a lamina protein. The preservation of cells through time conserve their organization, which needs the preservation of a healthy nuclear lamina. Senescent cells present failures in the nuclear lamina.

Nucleolus

Is a chromosomic entity, product of the transcriptional activity of specific genes. It could happen to be more than one nucleolus. It produces ribosomal subunits.

Cells in proliferation and active protein synthesis have larger nucleolus than inactive cells. The number of nucleolus varies but is cell type specific (more or less), from 2 to 5.

The rDNA sequence

In both extremes of the chromosome there are telomeric sequences. The centromeric sequence is necessary for chromosomal organization. There are many ribosomal genes (rDNA), which are sequencies that repeat. The human chromosome 1 is not nucleolar (i.e. that have these ribosomal sequences). These sequences are called NORs.

Chromosome 13, 14, 15, 21, 22 are the only human chromosomes that have rDNA. These means that, theoretically, there could be 10 nucleolus in a single cell. Nevertheless, these rDNA sequences can converge into producing a common nucleolus. Also, not all the NORs are expressed at the same time.

The number of nucleolus depend:

  • Number of nucleolar chromosomes.
  • Nucleolus demand.

RNA transcriptional machinery

Nucleolus in dispersion is called Christmass trees, which represent a huge machinery of RNA and ribonucleoproteins production associated to the NORs. Each one of these trees include:

  • Transcriptional factor UBF.
  • RNAPI.
  • Topoisomerases.

The transcriptional sense is always 5’ to 3’ in proteins, This determines that the copy sense es 3’ to 5’ (review this, I didn’t get it).

Ribosomal demand

The demand for ribosomes of a given cell is about 7 million yields per day (cells with great demand for protein synthesis). This demand is more or less fixed.

There is a process of gene amplification of rDNA. It produces a ring of DNA for extra ribosomal production.

Ribosomal RNA processing

What’s transcribed is a segment that produces a RNA 45S, which is later processed in rRNA 18S, 5.8S and 28S.

The modification of the primary transcript (rRNA 45S) is done through the marking of the sequence by snRNAs. These markings are then recognized by other nucleoproteins that cleave the rRNA into the smaller rRNAs. Then, ribosomal subunits are assembled from these parts and some previously created proteins called snRNPs.

Cajal bodies are sites where snRNPs ans snoRNPs undergo their final modifications. Interchromatin granule clusters are storage sites for fully mature snRNPs.

There are important extra components, like the rRNA 5S that do not correspond to any NOR (it comes from chromosome 1, and is present in many copies), but then is synthetized at the nucleolus formed by the NORs.

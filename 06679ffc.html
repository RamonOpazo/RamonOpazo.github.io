Nervous system development

The lecture aimed to explain that organ development and cell differentiation are system problems that arises from the interaction of several ambiance characteristics with a cell that has the potency to differentiate. This ambient characteristics are numerous and varying in quality. The second part of the class was originally about the application of these concepts to nervous system development, which was not lectured in the end.

Development of organs and tissues

There are two classical views from ancient Greece concerning embryonic development: predetermined shape of organs and, purpose of organ formation. In turn, this defined two ideas: preformism and epigenesis. Later, the same views were renamed mechanistic reductionism and the regulative (or integral) development.

In any way, both views were originally treated as a black box problem. In a practical manner, these views were later tested with the removal and the transplant of components in order to study deviation or lack thereof of normal development.

Deterministic development

Wilhelm Roux pierced one cell of a two-cell frog embryo and discovered that the survival blastomere formed only half of the body. This type of development is seen in invertebrates primarily (the classical example corresponds to Caenorhabtides elegans). Cell lineage was, then, a clear predetermined situation.

Regulative development

Interaction between components modify the development. Systems in this case are capable of adaptation to interference.

Hans Driesch studied sea urchin embryos. He dissociated the cells (not killed them) producing new whole organisms, which pointed that there should be no predetermination to cells. This development is common in vertebrates.

Ross Harrison transplanted half of double limb buds, observing normal development, informing that the structure developed in accordance to its surroundings.

The induction phenomenon

The blastopore, a pigmented region, indicated the zone were the embryo axis folded inside. The transplantation of this pore induced the formation a new embryo. The new embryo formed with the host’s cells and not from the pore ones.

The induction process means that a tissue changes its structure after being induced by another tissue. It requires signals, normally proteins released to the medium, arriving by diffusion to the target tissue, binding to receptors on the cell surface producing gene expressions that differentiate the cell to another line. Competency is the capability of the host tissue to respond to the signal, meaning expressing the receptor and all the other components needed for tissue formation. Cells change windows of competency due to time passing.

Diferentiation

The differentiation process silence genes, restricting the lineage of the cell, progressively, due to epigenetic changes. As such, the transition from pluripotency to fate specification follows the order:

graph LR;
    Totipotency --> Pluripotency --> Oligopotency --> Unipotency;

The development is, then, regulative in origin, and slowly turns into a deterministic one (differentiated cells). Stem cells of different tissues maintain certain degree of pluripotency in order to be able to renovate those tissues.

Positional information

The classical and central view on development was defined as the consequence of gene expression and its products.

Proposed by Lewis Wolpert, which posited that the positional information exposed cells to differing concentration gradients of molecules that determined the differentiation. Although some experiments were able to demonstrate a phenomenon akin to this concept, there are many counter examples.

Self-organization

Proposes that the differentiation is determined by the interaction of cells. Two molecules, one activator and another one inhibitor, diffuse at different paces, which in turn defines localized tissue differentiation.

Gastruloids are made by self-organization of embryonic stem cells. How is this possible, if all the cells were the same? So, how much of normal development is genetically determined, and how much correspond to self-organization?

Mechanics in development

An old idea that Newtonian forces is responsible for organic structure. Just recently was it possible to demonstrate this. The phenomenon is called cellular mechanotransduction, which is based on conformational changes that can be transfered to the cytoskeleton, which in turn changes the expression of genes inside a given cell.

An experiment where mesenchymal stem cells in vitro were positioned over an elastic substrate. Changing the elastic constant to values close to differentiated tissues induced the cell to differentiate towards that same tissue:

graph TD;
    A1[Brain] -->|1kPa| A2((Neuron));
    B1[Muscle] -->|10kPa| B2((Myocyte));
    C1[Collagenous bone] -->|100kPa| C2((Osteoblast));

The substrate geometry guides the migratory behavior of cells. There are microchips that expose a particular geometry and mechanical ambiance that allow cells to express differentiated tissues.

Embryo development, and in particular gastrulation, is a geometrical problem, with a mechanical solution, implemented biochemically. —Alfondo Martínez-Arias
